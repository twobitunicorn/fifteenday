# fifteenday

Public records belong to all of us. Almost nobody uses them.

Want to know how the city is actually spending your money? Want to see who lobbied your councilor before that vote? In Oregon, you can ask. They have to answer within fifteen business days, or tell you why they won't. That's ORS 192 — one of the strongest public records laws in the country.

But writing the request is intimidating. Wrong custodian, wrong scope, surprise fee quote, you give up. So this incredible civic tool sits unused by everyone except journalists and lawyers.

We're building the on-ramp. AI interviews you about what you want to know, drafts a properly-scoped request, routes it to the right office, writes the fee-waiver paragraph if you qualify under the public-interest provision — which almost nobody knows exists.

And every request, every response that comes back, gets added to a shared public library. Redacted, searchable. So the next person doesn't start from zero.

We're going to file real requests today. The demo is live acknowledgments coming in during the showcase, and one hero response we get back same-day.

Honest framing: we're technical Portlanders who care. Not lawyers, not journalists. The Oregon Public Records Advocate, MuckRock, SPJ Oregon — that's Monday's email. We're shipping the scaffold and putting it in the hands of the people who do this work for a living.

What I need: someone strong on structured generation and routing logic, someone comfortable with PDF parsing and a redaction pipeline — that's the riskiest piece. A frontend person who can ship a clean interview UI. And anyone who's ever filed a public records request or received one — your instincts beat my code.

Oregon's public records law belongs to everyone. Let's make using it together.

**Come build with me.**
